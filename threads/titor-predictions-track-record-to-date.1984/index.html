You're confusing two separate issues here, the "classical" version of Creutzfeldt-Jakob Disease is something that has been around forever in older people (it appears spontaneously at a rate of about one person per million per year, basically all in people in their 50s or older), but "mad cow disease" refers to a specific variant of this called Bovine Spongiform Encephalopathy (BSE) which affects cows and which can apparently be transmitted to humans who eat them. This variant is different from the usual version of CJD because it can affect younger people as well, that was what alerted doctors in Britain that something new was going on.

See this article, for example:

http://www.eatwelleatsafe.ca/illness/bse.htm

This article also says that BSE has an incubation time of 4-5 years in cattle, I don't know what it would be for humans. Given the suddenness of the outbreak in Britain, though, I'd assume that if there were a huge number of people in america with BSE, we'd have seen at least a few cases of people in their 20s or 30s showing symptoms by now.

E=mc^2, friend Solokin1. A very basic principle of our modern age.

Light does not possess mass itself, but light is energy, and energy is equivalent to mass. It is a strange state of affairs, and I lack a clear intuitive understanding of it myself, but I'll try to help you out.

Gravity bends light because gravity is a curvature of space-time. From a great distance, light appears to curve or bend near a source of gravity. But from the light's own perspective, it moves in a straight line the whole time. This is a relativistic effect. It is hard to describe accurately, but imagine this: the old rubber sheet analogy. Space is a rubber sheet, and a line drawn on the sheet represents a beam of light. Place a mass on the rubber sheet near the line. The line will appear to bend, but it will bend in a direction other than those allowed for by the two dimensions of the sheet. The case with gravity is the same. It is a non-euclidean curvature, meaning that it does not follow the obvious laws of geometry. That's that.

As to your solar sail question: the principal behind it is that the light exerts a force upon the sail, evoking movement in the opposite direction. The directional momentum of the light is transferred to the sail, but quite slowly. The effect on the light is that it loses energy, dropping to a longer wavelength. It would take quite a while to accelerate in a ship powered by a solar sail.

I assure you that light does not possess infinite energy. The energy carried by light is both finite, and easily measurable. If the energy of light were infinite, it would instantly destroy all mass in the universe and convert it into more light. This would pose quite a paradox, because if light possessed infinite energy then all mass would have to be inifinite in order to be converted into any energy at all. So what would happen? If it took time for mass to be converted into energy, then it would never be accomplished because there would be endless energy to extract. The mass would never be depleted though, because it too would be infinite. How would you fit infinite mass into a finite and measurable volume? (A black hole is a different case. I won't go there for now.)

Hope this has been some help, Solokin1. iqbal

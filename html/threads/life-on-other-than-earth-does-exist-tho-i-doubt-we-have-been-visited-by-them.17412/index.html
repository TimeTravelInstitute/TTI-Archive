Sorry to disappoint, but this has not been demonstrated to be true at all. It has been widely proposed, and it is a useful thought experiment to try when attempting to comprehend the idea of a fourth dimension. However, the most recent experiments (forgive me for not keeping the dates/names of reasearchers/techniques used on hand) have shown that the universe possesses neither convex (spherical) nor concave (saddle-shaped or hyperbolic) curvature. The geometry of space-time is, most probably, perfectly flat; i.e. the force of gravity is exactly balanced by the 'cosmological constant'. What this means is, gee golly here's a mind-trip, space is literally infinite. You could travel for all time and never reach an edge or come back to your starting point.

However - and this is important - the possible quantum states that any given volume of space can come to embody in 13.7 billion years is quite finite. Therefore, once you travelled a great enough distance (many trillions of light years probably), the quantum arrangements would start to repeat. (Ignore the fact that you'd be in gross excess of c, this is hypothetical). That means, you would come to an exact copy of Earth. You might find an exact copy of yourself there, or that copy might already have left on a journey across the universe just like you did. In the intervening space you would have crossed, you would necessarily have come across a great variety of alien life, since you would have crossed an extent of space containing roughly the cube root of all possible quantum states. Of course, you could never actually reach that copy of Earth, nor could you reach all but maybe the closest of those life-supporting planets. You simply can't travel fast enough. By the time you reached the alternate Earth, its sun would have exploded, its matter decayed, and it might have even had time to dissipate as a cloud of infrared radiation... and so would you.

I'm sure a few of you are saying "wait! Those images from the hubble are showing places in the universe that they're saying are closer and closer to a point at which everything becomes opaque!" Well, that's almost right. They're approaching being able to see light from a time at which the universe was opaque. It is confusing for many people when they hear that the universe is 13.7 billion light years across. It makes it sound like it has an edge. At that kind of distance, stretches of space are equivalent to stretches of time. Right now, space 13.7 billion LY away looks pretty much like space in our neck of the universe looks: middle aged. There is a difference between the observable universe and the Universe. The value of that difference verges on infinity.

So, I would tend to agree with Wedgie. The chances that any aliens have had time to reach Earth is virtually nil, especially if we're assuming they will be crossing space like an ordinary rocket, with no shortcuts. My opinion is that any technological civilization advanced enough to develop interstellar travel will probably be advanced enough to devise a way around needing to use interstellar travel, whether through messing with the topology of spacetime, or through accepting their place on the planet they call home.
